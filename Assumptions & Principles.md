THE THEORY OF DOMAIN‑DEPENDENT FAILURE

Alpha Assumption — Domain Permission Assumption
Data collection only happens when the domain allows it to happen.  
If the domain blocks perception — through conditions, context, mindset, environment, or stimulus — the observer cannot collect data, even if information is present.

Examples:

A sensor cannot detect heat if the domain (temperature range) is outside its capability.

A human cannot notice danger if their mindset domain is distracted.

A robot cannot see objects if lighting domain changes.

A business cannot detect risk if its reporting domain hides the signal.

***********************************************************************************************************************************************************
This assumption explains why “information everywhere” does not equal “data everywhere.”
I. Foundational Assumptions
These are the bedrock truths your theory rests on.

Assumption 1 — Reality is continuous.
The world is always changing, regardless of whether anyone observes it.

Assumption 2 — Information exists everywhere.
Every object, state, and event contains information at all times.

Assumption 3 — Data is not reality; data is captured information.
Data only exists when an observer (human, machine, robot) captures part of reality.

Assumption 4 — Perception is domain‑dependent.
An observer can only perceive information that is visible within their current domain.

Assumption 5 — A domain is any condition that shapes perception.
A domain can be:

mindset

time

temperature

weather

disaster

emotional state

environment

purpose

technology

context

Anything that changes what the observer can see, detect, or understand.

Assumption 6 — Domains shift continuously.
Domains are not static; they drift, evolve, and transform.

Assumption 7 — Agents have limited focus.
Humans, robots, and machines cannot track all domains simultaneously.

****************************************************************************************************************************************************************************************************
II. Core Principles of the Theory
These are the governing laws that emerge from the assumptions.

Principle 1 — Visibility Principle
Data becomes visible only when the observer enters a domain where that information is meaningful.

Information is everywhere, but visibility is domain‑specific.

Principle 2 — Domain Alignment Principle
An agent succeeds when its perception aligns with the domain it is operating in.

Alignment = awareness + relevance + focus.

Principle 3 — Domain Drift Principle
Failure occurs when the domain changes faster than the agent adapts.

Examples:

weather shifts

market shifts

emotional shifts

environmental shifts

sensor calibration shifts

The agent is still focused — but the domain has moved.

Principle 4 — Focus Loss Principle
Failure also occurs when the agent loses focus on the domain, even if the domain stays the same.

Examples:

distraction

fatigue

complacency

sensor noise

cognitive overload

The domain is stable — but the agent drifts.

Principle 5 — Dual Failure Law
**All failures reduce to one of two causes:

domain change, or

loss of domain focus.**

This is the unifying law of your theory.

Principle 6 — Domain Relativity Principle
There is no universal start or end of data — only domain‑relative beginnings and endings.

A recycled product has many “lifecycles,” depending on the domain observing it.

Principle 7 — Stimulus Activation Principle
A domain shift is triggered by any stimulus that alters perception.

Stimuli include:

temperature

time

mindset

weather

disaster

purpose

technology

context

A tiny stimulus can create a massive domain shift.

Principle 8 — Domain Blindness Principle
An observer inside a domain cannot perceive the data of another domain until they transition into it.

This explains:

why people miss obvious insights

why robots fail in new lighting

why safety incidents occur

why analysts overlook patterns

why organizations collapse during change

III. The Grand Summary (Signature Statement)

“Information is everywhere, but data is domain‑dependent.
Failure occurs when the agent and the domain fall out of alignment.”

This is clean, universal, and original.
